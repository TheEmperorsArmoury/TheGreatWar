Game Concept

Concept Overview: is an RTS that immerses players in the strategic and tactical challenges of World War I. The first iteration, the race to develop a polished working demo, will be single player. But a responsive, eSports level multiplayer will be the goal if the game recieves funding. The gameplay will emphasie a balance between economy, strategy and real time action.

Inspiration: Gameplay is heavily inspired by Age of Empires II and Knights of Honour. The artistic and thematic style is heavily inspired by the Grimdark aesthetic frequently found in Warhammer 40,000.

Core Gameplay: Players assume the roles of historical factions, orchestrating large-scale military operations on the battlegrounds of Europe. The game features large strategic decisions like research, production and moving regiments, and close up fast tactical play.

Unique Selling Points:

  • Fresh Experience: WWI is a very underrepresented theatre in gaming, only 1 in every 36 RTS games are about WWI (approx). This gives players a much wider range of new units, tactics and experiences to explore than many RTS games offer. This provides a young game studio a blank canvas to work with.
  • Strategic Depth: A lot of moving parts that are packaged neatly into 2 distinct map scales and 4 distinct tactical stages. Both small decisions and large decisions can have meaningful impact on the outcome.
  • Olde World Charm, Modern Mechanical Monstrosities: The WWI period intersected pre-industrial equipment and tactics with the horrors of death delivered at a new industrial scale. This game will capture the blend of old and new in a way not seen before. A potent cocktail of cavalry charges and mustard gas, Napoleonic era formations and analogue targeting computers. Unique!

Gameplay Mechanics

The game loop is divided between the macro and micro management of 2 maps and 4 General Orders that run in real time. The 2 maps cover different scales of the battlefield, a large view of whole regions or a granular view of specific battlefields, zooming with the mouse roller will switch scales. The stages of General Orders are a more abstract concept but are basically like "ages" in Age of Empires, levels of advancement that have requirements to reach and provide new opportunities at each stage. General Orders cannot be undone once called up. There is no limitation on where anything can be built or moved but there are sensible places to build and move. Like building barracks in urban areas or steel foundries close to iron deposits is a good idea. But there might be good reasons to build or move near or in enemy areas, like field hospitals or incursions behind enemy lines. Here is a more detailed description of the game loops sections:

Maps


Macro Scale: Strategic Map

This is high level map view covers whole regions for macro management. A player uses this map view to manage production, research and moving entire armies. At this scale, the player handles economy like workers, resource management, production of units and logistics (roads, rail, shipping). The strategic map view will handle nearly all the economy game elements with very few military elements, just strategic bombing.

Micro Scale: Tactical Map

This detailed map view covers specific battlefields for micro management. The player has tight, responsive control over units. The player will command individual units to collect supplies, dig (gun pits, trenches, dugouts, tunnels), build (wire, lay mines, lookout posts, machine gun nests), move or attack. Using hotkeys will be crucial. Most of the military game elements are handled at this scale with very few economy elements handled here, just wood chopping for lumber. There is an additional layer to this map which is that players can engage in tunnel warfare by digging through 3 subterranean layers under the battlefield.

General Orders

The stages of the game are called "General Orders". Each order affects economic and military elements. Although the button to call up the next General Order is accessed through the capital city located on the Strategic Map, the order will affect all areas of the game. General Orders are as follows:

1: Mobilisation

Focus on rapidly gathering essential resources and swiftly advancing to the next General Order. Efficient resource management and early strategic planning are key.

2: Fortification

Prioritize the construction of critical defence structures and expand your military forces. Launch probing attacks to gauge enemy strength, positions and attempt to hamper their rate of expansion.

3: Assault

Launch aggressive attacks to weaken and dislodge enemy forces. Simultaneously, continue expanding your military and economy, advance in technology research and secure strategically advantageous positions on the battlefield.

4: Hold the Line

The battle reaches its crescendo with full-scale onslaughts on multiple fronts. Finalise research on remaining technologies to ensure your units have superior capabilities. This is the last desperate, all out struggle to win the game.

To call up the next General Order the player needs to pay certain resources, have certain buildings and unlock certain technologies. The requirement for the next General Order increases but so do the rewards of each General Order, access to new and better units and buildings, accesses to new and better upgrades on existing units and buildings. Calling up the next General Order before your opponent will often put a player at an advantage but there may be some situations/strategies where spending resources on increasing economy or military is more advantageous than calling up the next General Order.

Economy and Research

Economy

As typical in every single RTS under the sun, will have an ingame economic system. There will be dedicated economic units like civilians but soldiers will also act as econmic units for wood and supplies. The resources are:

  • Wood

    This can be collected at home or at the front. Economic units like civilians can collect it but so can soldiers where forests are available at the front. Lumber mill is the required building.

  • Steel

    Collected by economic units like civilians from patches of iron ore. A foundry is the required building to drop off iron and increase steel.

  • Supplies

    Supplies are very similar to food in standard RTS games but will deviate a little and will make our game unique. It includes food, but will also include ammo, fuel and medical supplies. Just like food in AOEII there will be multiple sources of supplies like oil fields, farms, pharmacies, ammo factories etc. They might have specific bonuses and/or effects, we can decide when trying to balance the game.

    As an additional difference to most RTS games "food" resource, the full game should treat supply depots as a resource field, including enemy supply depots. Soldiers will have to ferry supplies between a supply depot and their station, like artillery guns or trenches. Enemy supplies can be collected as long as your units can get to it. This is a cool, unique and historically accurate feature.

  • Propaganda

    Propaganda will be the core resource to recruit more infantry, civilians and call up the next General Order. It will be generated by media organizations.

    2 additional features of propaganda:

    1. It will increase morale which buffs military and economic units.
    2. A propaganda-generating unit (journalist, upgraded to war correspondent) will create more propaganda by being near civilians when they are being killed.

  • Debt

    This represents national debt. A player will start with a bank and bankers (Redshield faction) and can create debt right from the beginning. Debt can purchase any other resource or can be used to advance to the next General Order, Using debt to purchase resources will cause inflation which is a higher price for resources.

    Although it seems like debt is free, in fact it decreases the players score, so a player can actually loose the match by falling too deep into debt, even if they are winning on the battlefield. Debt will debuff a the economic units and buildings, your economy will be crippled by debt and your factories will grind to a halt under too much dept. It's a resource that if managed carefully can boost a player at strategic moments, but loose him sthe game is mismanaged.

    It may take a lot of effort to get this game mechanic to balance properly, it's unique and we won't have other games to reference for this feature of .

    Debt cannot be repaid.

Units and Buildings

The units and buildings will be symetrical across factions but there will be some unique units to add flavour. This game will again stick closley to the AOEII architecture but with a few exceptsion.

Units

Stick close to AOEII for inspiration and balance of units. Notable exception is the Flying Korps.

    Civilians. They are assigned to do jobs like in AOEII, they can be assulted directly impacting their ability to work, this is a viable strategy to win.

  • Infantry

    The standard unit of the game, Comes in differnet unit types like rifleman, stormtrooper, mortar team and machinegunners.

  • Mounted

The size dictates range, speed and blast, small guns can be moved quickly but have the least range and blast, large guns have the longest range, largest blast but can be moved the slowest. All guns should require some time to set up and tear down between movement. Larger guns will have such range that they can be kept at a safe distance from the front line trenches.

  • Flying Korps

Buildings

Buildings will fall under 3 catagories: economic, production and military:

  • Economic

    These buildings will be closely related to resource collection, such as:

    • Town Center: Produces Labour Corps Workers, the standard economic unit, aka civilians
    • Lumber Mill: The drop off point for wood collection.
    • Foundry: The drop off point for iron collection.
    • Refinery, Mill, Munitions Plant: The drop off point for oil, food and chemical which are all types of supplies for collection.
    • Media Office: The drop off point of opinion collected by civilians assigned to work there. Opinion is collected from locations where friendly civilians are at, in effect the civilians are the resource field.
    • Banks: These dont't collect any resource but if they're destroyed the player cannot leverage debt unless rebuilt.
  • Production

    These buildings will be closely related to unit production, such as:

    • Drill Hall: Produces infantry.
    • Stable: Produces mounted units.
    • War Factory: Produces guns.
    • Aerodrome: Produces flying corps units.
  • Military

    These buildings can be thougth of as immobile units, with firepower and strength but stationary. They are the single most powerful units in the game. They are:

    • Trenches: The most basic military building, used extensivley, built by soldiers and upgraded with wire and machinegun nests. Can only be used as defensive structures. They are constructed at the tactical map scale by soldiers.
    • Dugout: A fairly common secure addition to trench networks allows your troops to survive incoming fire. Tougher and greater capacity than trenches but weaker and less firepower than a bunker. Also defensive structures. They are constructed at the tactical map scale by soldiers.
    • Bunker: A fortified structure offering superior protection and firepower. More robust and durable than dugouts, bunkers can withstand intense artillery bombardment. Fully armed they provide a formidable installation within or near trench networks, significantly enhancing your troops' survivability and combat effectiveness in the nearby area. Can be used defensivley but also built in an aggressive forward position, consolidating gains made in rapid advances. They are constructed at the tactical map scale by soldiers.
    • Fortress: The single largest, most expensive and powerful unit in . Placing down a fortress is a powerful move and can win the game. They can be placed defensivly a little from enemy lines to ensure breakthroughs are impossible or can be built aggressivly toward or even inside enemy lines (if can actually manage) to create very tough positions for the enemy to assult, giving your side a significant advantage in that region. However aggressively building fortresses might cost you the game if you fail to capitalise on the gain, given how expenisive a fortress is to build and how many lives it might cost to construct it in a forward offensive position. They are constructed at the strategic map scale by soldiers and labour corps (civilians).
    • Tunnels: Not strictly a building but a critical aspect of , tunnels are constructed at the tactical map scale by specialized engineer units. They serve multiple purposes: surprise attacks behind enemy lines and destroying enemy fortifications through sapping and counter sapping actions. Tunnels can dramatically alter the battlefield by allowing for surprise assaults, either blowing up a trench strong point to support ground level assaults or being an offensive action in their own right by destroying key military buildings like forts. Their construction is slow but can yield significant tactical advantages, especially in breaking stalemates or destroying strategic points.

Development Roadmap

The project's objective is to construct a polished and playable demo within an eight-month timeframe. Among the various sections of this Game Design Document, the development roadmap stands out for its flexibility. This roadmap will serve as a useful guide rather than a rigid plan, as it breaks down the game's complex challenges into smaller, modular problems. These can be solved independently and then integrated into the main solution. The initial step in tackling these challenges involves dividing the work into two primary phases: the prototype phase and the demo phase. Notably, certain elements of the working demo, such as cutscenes or 3D models, can be developed in parallel with the prototype, even before its completion. These components, while not essential for a robust prototype, can be progressed concurrently, resources permitting. As such, while the roadmap's steps are presented in a linear fashion, the actual development process will be much more parallel in nature. The success of this approach hinges on the project manager's expertise in effectively dividing tasks and overseeing their progression. The following are the initial divisions outlined in the roadmap:

Prototype Development

In developing RTS, the cornerstone of our initial efforts will be the creation of a very lean, very performant prototype. This prototype will serve as the project's foundation, enabling us to test core gameplay mechanics, explore the game's unique features, and refine the overall player experience. Central to this phase is the focus on key RTS elements such as unit control, resource management, and typical NPC agent behavior, ensuring they are not only functional but engaging too. We will prioritise a player-centric design approach, where feedback is actively solicited and incorporated from early playtests, even if in the early stages we are the only play testers. This iterative process will allow us to quickly identify and address any gameplay or technical issues. In the prototype phase of , our primary focus will be on crafting sound game mechanics, the backbone of an engaging player experience. This involves meticulously designing and iterating on core gameplay elements. The prototype phase will not be complete until we create a mechanically robust and intuitive game system that emphasizes strategic depth and player skill.

To make best use of time, programmers will only concern themselves with the bare minimum aesthetics to ensure we can develop the prototype, all the polishing will be segregated to the demo phase. Creating an AI opponent should also be postponed to the demo phase as it's not needed for core gameplay mechanics, the focus of the prototype phase. Here are the components essential for the prototype:

  • GameState: The keystone upon which the whole game is built.

    The game state is potentially the single most important element in the whole game. The game state, in essence, is the actual game itself. It is the place where all the moving parts come together in one place. The maps, weather conditions, resources collected, buildings, units and even artillery shells in mid-flight will all be stored in the game state. All the rest of the programming revolves around interacting with the game state, whether it's moving units, drawing maps, pausing/saving, delivering event-based audio, etc. The game state is the single source of data upon which the rest of the game's engineering feeds. We need to store the data in 3 data structures which are:

    1. 2D Array: Static, Evenly Distributed Objects

      Many objects in the game are static, rarely, if ever, moving. This includes elements like terrain tiles and forests, which are uniformly distributed across the map. These objects do not lend themselves to rapid iteration; instead, they require sequential processing. A 2D array is an ideal solution for managing these static elements. It inherently aligns each object's position with its corresponding location on the game map — for instance, an object at array(0,0) corresponds to the game map coordinates of (0,0). This natural alignment of data structure and spatial distribution simplifies the management of static, evenly distributed objects such as:

      • Maps (Strategic, Tactical, Subterranean)
      • Terrain
      • Some Scenery (Large sections like forrests)
      • Fog of War
    2. Quad Tree: Dynamic Unevenly Distributed Objects

      In contrast to static objects, dynamic, unevenly distributed objects in the game, such as moving units or variable resources, are optimally managed with a Quad Tree data structure. This approach is particularly effective in real-time strategy (RTS) games, where handling hundreds of units simultaneously is common. The Quad Tree dynamically adjusts, expanding and contracting based on the current positions and statuses of these objects. This flexibility is essential for efficiently managing a constantly changing game state.

      The GameStateManager class plays a pivotal role in this process, ensuring that the game state remains as efficient as possible. For instance, when a non-player character (NPC) moves, the Agent class requests an update from the GameStateManager. The manager then evaluates whether there's an existing quadrant in the Quad Tree for the NPC. If not, it generates one immediately. Conversely, it collapses empty quadrants back into their parent nodes to maintain optimal efficiency. This dynamic handling, delegated to the specialised the GameStateManager class, ensures the game state serves its primary function: storing an efficient and current snapshot of the game. The main properties stored by the game state in a Quad Tree are:

      • Units (Military, Economic)
      • Most Scenery (Buildings, Trenches, Wire etc)
      • Projectiles (Artillery shells, flames etc)
      • Available Resources
    3. Streamlined Storage for Simple Game Elements

      Some elements of the game only require a very simple data structure to store efficiently. Probably just an array or key:value map will be enough. Game elements that can be stored like this are:

      • Collected Resources
      • Technology Tree Status
      • Score Counter
  • GameStateManager: Dynamically manages the game state.
    This will be built as the GameState class also gets built. Because the game state will only store data a manager will be needed to keep it up to date, that is the purpose of this manager. Everything that writes to the game state will pass through the GameStateManager to ensure the game state's structure is optimised at all times.
  • AnimationManager(start): Absolute minimum graphics rendering for prototype visualisation.

    Work on this class will be divided between the prototype and demo phase. For the prototype, we just need the most basic, essential visualisations so we can ensure the other components of the game are working correctly, like controlling units, placing buildings, UI, etc. The AnimationManager class will store the map 2D array data from the GameState class. That data will provide the spatial coordinates of map tiles the AnimationManager needs to render terrain tiles in the correct position. Here are the essential functions of the AnimationManager class:

    • Render Terrain: This aspect focuses on accurately rendering the terrain to create a visually coherent and realistic game environment. For the prototype, the emphasis is on ensuring correct representation of terrain heights and designating impassable areas. 3 levels of terrain will exist, 1 strategic map which will have a medium amount of detail, 2 tactical map which will have the maximum amout of detail and 3 the subterranean view which is the most basic of all. Exact details will be handled during development.
    • Render Scenery: The most basic scenery elements need to be rendered to ensure the correct funtionality of the prototype. Scerny is buildings, roads, trenches and trees, objects that are not units but can still interact. The initial version of scenery will just be an obsticle for pathfinding. Spatial coordinates of scenery peices will be stored in the GameState class.
    • Render Units: Like terrain and scenery, units will be stored in the GameState and that info used to position them correctly on the screen. As the game state changes, so too will the position of units. In the very begining we won't bother with an animated spritesheet, still sprites will suffice. But animating the sprites will still be built in the prototype, just later in the phase, maybe once 3d artists have drawn a full spritesheet.
    • Map Scale Switching: Ensures that the correct map scale is displayed based on user input. This feature allows for a seamless transition between different zoom levels, allowing the player to switch between the stategic map, tactical map or subterranean views.
    • Culling: Refers to the process where objects outside the player's current perspective are not rendered. This technique is crucial for maintaining high performance and ensuring smooth gameplay by reducing the load on graphics processing.
    • Minimap: Once a basic UI is working, a mini map will be displayed in it. This mini map will display a representation of the map view available to the player, whether strategic, tactical or tunnels. The mini map will dispay abreviated info about the main map view, like unit positions, resources, trenches etc. This will follow the very standard mini map design found across nearly all RTS games.
  • UserInputManager: Manages the input from the user.
    This will include the hotkeys, mouse actions like clicks, scroller and movement. We will not bother to create a way to change hotkeys during prototyping, leave that to the full game development.
  • Simple UI: Bare minimun to interact with prototype.
    Most basic user interface components needed for player interaction. This will be 2 sets of widgets 1 running along the top of the window displaying resource stats. The 2nd, more important interface section will run along the botton of the window which will show unit abilities, selected unit info, mini map and leave room for in game chat. We should not get too creative about the UI, AOEII has a very good UI and we should use it as a close reference.
  • Agents: Basic NPC scripts.

    This is just the most basic level of AI required for units to display "normal" behavior. Like setting stances, if a player's unit is set to "aggressive" stance, if an enemy unit comes within range the player's unit will attack the enemy unit and actively follow the enemy even if that goes outside the original range.

    Or "normal" RTS NPC behavior of workers, who go back and forth between the resource they've been assigned to and the drop off point for their resource, like wood cutters dropping off at a lumber mill or tractors dropping off at farmhouse.

    This section is where the pathfinding will be managed. It's a very important section. It needs to be built with a modular interface that will be used with either the player through input or used by the AI. This modular approach will allow us to easily update the agent's, the AI or the user interface.

  • SkirmishManager: Handle Hitboxes
    Manages small-scale conflicts or battles in the game.
  • Game Loop: Runs the game.
    Needs to run on time delta to ensure game time is accurate.

Story and Setting

Story mode will be a feature to implement if there is time, single player skirmish gameplay will be the primary focus. Campaign and multiplayer will be added if time allows.

The storyline serves as a backdrop to the gameplay, enriching the player's experience without overpowering it. Emphasis is placed on robust game mechanics and strategic depth, ensuring engaging gameplay. While the story offers context and emotional depth, it is the strategic and tactical choices that drive the game, allowing players to immerse themselves in the challenges of command during The Great War.

Many war games are either amoral or throw morality out of the window in favour of propaganda tropes. The storyline of this game will address morality directly and place the blame squarely on the shoulders of the politicians who dragged their nations into yet more pointless wars. The story will not be propagandistic but it will also not shy away from the blood guilt incurred by the leaders of that time. Nations themselves will not be painted as good guys or bad guys in the campaign. Real historic reference material will be used as inspiration for the story.

The game should not be pinned down by historical accuracy but use history as inspiration, heavy artistic licence can be used if needed to improve the gaming experience. The player should have maximum agency to impact the course of the game, therefore there must be scope for an outcome that differs from reality.

If you fancy an entertaining overview of the narrative style, check this video about The Seige of Vraks in Warhammer 40,000.

Again, story mode will only be built if time allows, it's not the primary focus, gameplay is. This gives plenty of space for DLCs.

Art Style

The art style of can be summed up in a word quite easily: Grimdark atmosphere, dieselpunk detail. It strikes a balance between historical accuracy and fun aesthetics. While maintaining accurate proportions of units, buildings, and scenery, the details are rendered in a stylised, somewhat cartoonish quality. This is complemented by thematic shades, diverging from strict realism to enhance the game's atmosphere. The skies and horizons are particularly dramatic, styled with dark, ominous features that create impactful backdrops, evoking the intense emotions of war. This stylistic choice aims to make the game visually appealing and engaging while preserving gravitas. For visual inspiration and a better understanding of this artistic direction, check out this video showcasing the Death Guard legion, which exemplifies a similar blend of realism and stylisation. When polishing the game we should add some exciting lighting and screen shake effects on explosions and eerie effects of gas clouds creeping over battlefields. A day/night/season cycle and weather should be a visual representation of certain game mechanics like visibility, gas weapons and terrain movement effects (hard or soft mud based on season and weather).

Since the primary focus of is on mechanical gameplay, the development of the art style will be a subsequent phase. The initial stages of the project will concentrate on solidifying the core gameplay mechanics, ensuring they are engaging, functional, and well-tuned. Once the gameplay foundation is strong the art development will commence. This approach will ensure that the visual elements complement and enhance the established mechanics, rather than overpower them. Because there will be many units onscreen at any one time, the computational load of visuals will be handled economically to ensure performance doesn't tank.

Once the mechanical aspects of the prototype are well underway, digital artists/3D Modellers will be sought to handle game art. Their role will be to create the feel of game aesthetics, create high-poly models and convert to sprite sheets, and create animations for presenting the demo to players and investors.

Sound and Music

Sound Design

  • Sound Engineer: Similar to handling art during the demo development phase, a sound engineer will be sought to contribute his expertise to the project after the core game mechanics are solidly in place. His role will involve crafting a compelling and immersive audio landscape that enhances gameplay and narrative. This includes creating and sourcing sound effects, ambient sounds, and potentially working on the game's musical score. The sound engineer is expected to bring his own specialized tools and resources to the project, and collaborate closely with the development team to ensure the audio aligns with the game's artistic vision and gameplay dynamics.
  • Prioritization of Sound Effects: During development, the highest priority is given to effects that directly enhance gameplay mechanics. This approach ensures that sounds crucial for player feedback, incoming artillery fire or your units carrying out orders, are developed first to aid in strategic decision-making and improve the overall gaming experience.
  • Event Driven Sound Effects: Tailored audio cues that give players vital information about in-game events, enhancing gameplay awareness and immersion through responsive, contextual sounds. For example, a distant siren indicating an impending attack, enhancing the player's situational awareness.
  • Context-Sensitive Ambient Sounds: Trigger specific ambient sound effects based on the player's location and the events happening around them. For example, near a battlefield, the sounds of distant artillery fire could be heard, whereas in a base camp, the focus might shift to the chatter of soldiers and the clanking of equipment.
  • Multilingual Voice Acting for Factions: Each faction's voice acting is in its national language, enhancing authenticity and appealing to players' cultural identity, particularly in non-English speaking countries. This approach heightens immersion and personal connection to the game.

Music

  • Event Driven Background Tracks: Employ more upbeat music during the final General Order of Hold The Line.
  • Royalty-Free Music: Use royalty-free music tracks available online. Websites like Incompetech or Free Music Archive offer a variety of tracks that can be used for free or with attribution.
  • Minimalist Approach: Minimalist approach to music. Sometimes, less is more – a few well-chosen tracks can be effective for setting the mood.
  • Public Domain Historical Music: Research and incorporate music from the era that is now in the public domain. Early 20th-century compositions might be available for use without licensing issues.

Environment Design

The design of environments in will lean on the landscapes and settings of World War I but prioritising the game's strategic and tactical gameplay over realism.

  • Historically Inspired Battlefields: Levels are based on real World War I battlefields, featuring authentic terrains like trenches and war-torn villages.
  • Interactive Environments: Environments are interactive, providing tactical advantages and depth to gameplay. For example, being on a hill increases view and weapon range.
  • Dynamic Weather and Time: Changing weather conditions and time-of-day cycles affect movement and visibility, adding strategic elements.
  • Destructible Elements: Certain scenery features are destructible, allowing dynamic battlefield changes.
  • Varied Scales and Perspectives: Different map scales offer a deeper gameplay experiences, from the large macro strategic map view, the detailed micro tactical map and an addiitonal scroll level to handle underground tunnel warfare.
  • Atmospheric Detailing: Small details like spent shell casings and makeshift encampments enhance the immersive atmosphere.

User Interface

The UI of will closely follow the established RTS UI paradigm for familiarity and ease of adoption by new players. Drawing inspiration from the efficient and user-friendly Age of Empires II UI system, we will replicate its effectiveness without unnecessary innovation. However, the aesthetic design will be tailored to fit the setting of our game, enhancing the visual appeal and overall gaming experience.

  • Mouse:
    • Cursor to Window Edge: Moving the cursor to the edge of the game window scrolls the view around the map, allowing players to navigate different areas seamlessly.
    • Left Button:
      • Select Units/Buildings: A single left-click selects a unit or building, displaying its options and information.
      • Drag Selection: Clicking and dragging selects multiple units, enabling group commands and management.
    • Right Button:
      • Issue Commands: A right-click directs selected units to move, attack, gather resources, or interact with the environment.
    • Scroll Wheel:
      • Zoom In/Out: Scrolling up or down zooms the camera in or out, changing perspective between the macro strategic map, the micro tactical map or underground tunnels and dugouts.
  • Keyboard:
    • [1-0] keys will be reserved for Unit Assignment.
    • [QWERT] keys will be reserved for Actions like "construction" or "attack".
    • [ASDFG] keys will be reserved for Stances like "aggressive", "defensive", "stand ground", etc.
    • [ZXCVB] keys will be reserved for Formations like "line", "tight box", "large box", etc.
    • General hotkeys Will be available for buildings and "jumping to" a view, like a particular part of a particular battlefield.

    The key functions will be nested, for example, selecting a worker or soldier will open all the options since they can perform all options, but a building cannot move, therefore the Formation options will not open when a building is selected. Or an artillery gun cannot build anything, so the Construction Action options will not open when an artillery gun is selected.

    The [QWERTASDFZXCV] keys and the number row have been strategically chosen for their ergonomic positioning, making them ideally suited for rapid and efficient hotkey-based gameplay. Their physical placement on the keyboard will align seamlessly with the 5x3 grid layout of the game's options menu, facilitating intuitive and swift navigation (refer to the accompanying image for visual layout).

  • On Screen Menu:

    The on-screen control menu is designed for accessibility, with options selectable via left mouse clicks and closable through right mouse clicks. While clicking on-screen buttons is conventional in RTS games and will be included for familiarity, it's acknowledged as less efficient for controlling gameplay. Therefore, the game will emphasize the use of hotkeys, guiding players to more efficient control methods while still providing the standard point-and-click interface.

  • Hotkey Scoping:

    Given the large number of units to manage, the standard 10 hotkeys for units may prove insufficient for fast and engaging micromanagement. To address this, while on the macro strategic map, direct unit control is not available, hotkeys focusing instead on broader strategic control like selecting cities, steel foundries or gun factories. Players can quickly switch to a specific micro tactical map using a dedicated hotkey [1-0]. In this tactical view, unit hotkeys become active but are localized to the currently viewed battlefield. This design ensures that each unit has a unique hotkey assignment within its respective tactical map, allowing for efficient management of numerous units without conflicts. Additionally, battlefield locations and hotkey assignments are stored as member properties of unit objects for seamless gameplay.

RTS Genre Trend

Rekindled: Analytics of a Genre Finding Renewed Fire

The real-time strategy (RTS) genre, once a titan of the gaming world, has undergone a fascinating transformation. While its market share and mainstream presence have undoubtedly shrunk compared to FPS and MOBA juggernauts, recent data hints at a subtle but promising resurgence. Let's delve into the numbers to paint a clearer picture.

Market Trends:
  • Declined, but now Stabilising: According to Newzoo, the RTS market saw a steady decline from 2012 to 2018, falling from 5% of the global PC market to just 1.5%. However, since then, it has stabilized, holding steady at around 1.4% in 2022. This suggests a core, dedicated audience that remains engaged.
  • Indie Spark: While AAA RTS releases have become rarer, the indie scene has stepped up. According to GameDev.net, the number of new RTS titles released annually has remained constant around 100-150 since 2018. This steady stream of innovation, often focusing on tighter mechanics and streamlined controls, is attracting new players and revitalizing the community.
Competitive Scene:
  • Esports: While not dominating the esports landscape as in its heydays, titles like StarCraft II and Age of Empires IV maintain vibrant competitive communities. Smaller titles like Iron Harvest are also building their competitive scenes, offering opportunities for dedicated RTS players.
  • Streaming: Twitch viewership for RTS games, while not in the top tiers, shows promise. According to StreamElements, StarCraft II consistently pulls in around 30,000 viewers, with Age of Empires IV and smaller titles like They Are Billions also garnering healthy audiences. This indicates continued interest and engagement from the core community.
Challenges and Potential:
  • Steep Learning Curve: RTS games remain notoriously challenging to master, with complex mechanics and demanding multitasking. This high barrier to entry can deter new players, limiting the genre's growth potential.
  • Console Conundrum: The traditional RTS control scheme, heavily reliant on mouse and keyboard, doesn't translate well to consoles, a massive gaming market segment. Innovative control solutions or genre-specific console games are crucial for broader reach.
Looking Ahead:

The RTS genre isn't experiencing a full-blown renaissance, but it's certainly far from dead. Dedicated developers are injecting fresh ideas, a passionate community keeps the flame alive, and data suggests a stable, engaged player base. Continued accessibility improvements, innovative control schemes, and captivating new approaches to classic mechanics hold the key to rekindling the RTS fire and attracting a wider audience. While navigating the challenges, the genre has the potential to carve out a unique and thriving space in the modern gaming landscape, proving that strategic depth and real-time empire building still hold a powerful allure for players seeking a cerebral and rewarding gaming experience.
